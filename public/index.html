The Deceptive type is the proof of concept: the baseline reads "competitor leverage" at face value, pushes harder, and the counterparty walks — $0 every run. Synod probes, disarms the deception, and closes the deal — $3,000 every run. On probing: in the deterministic council the Learning lens is hard-gated by the EVI rule (probe iff information value exceeds cost); in live mode the EVI is an advisory referee — the LLM worldviews may override it in either direction, and every divergence is named on screen and recorded in the round receipt. The ±$0 variance is structural, not accidental: both outcomes are reproducible because the decisive factor is whether the council probes, not counterparty randomness.

The Soft surface type shows the range advantage: Synod ($3,468 ± $98) captures more surplus with higher variance than the baseline ($2,906 ± $19) — the baseline's tighter spread reflects its single-stance predictability, which is exactly what the deceptive type punishes.

Removing the EVI probe trigger costs $1,584 of total surplus and collapses the Deceptive result to $1,423 — the probe rule earns its seat. The single-lens rows are the deeper argument: collapsing the council to one worldview spans $2,941 to $8,968 depending on which lens you bet on — and the winner is only knowable in hindsight. The full council matches the best single lens ex ante, without knowing which worldview the scenario will reward. Two honest nulls, reported as found: the causal-challenge concession rarely flips a round in the deterministic council (its force is real but small here; live-Qwen defenders concede on merit), and uniform weighting costs only $45 on this suite — the Arbiter's terrain reading shows up in confidence calibration more than raw surplus.
