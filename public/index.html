§02How it decides

It decides by disagreement.

Five judges look at the same move. They often disagree. A neutral chair weighs them and makes the call.

“Why not just ask one model to weigh five perspectives?”
Because these aren't five opinions. They're five different ways of judging. Three are pure math, not an AI. Probe calculates whether a question is worth asking. Hedge calculates the worst case. Trust calculates who they most likely are. In the live run, the five judges reason on Qwen. The math stays outside them, in code. Code tracks the evidence. Code weighs the five. Code makes the call. One model playing five voices gives five guesses from the same head. And a different answer every time. Synod gives the same answer every time — and shows you which judge changed it.

Exhibit B — is every part pulling its weight? — expandCut the check-the-claim step: −$1,606, the biggest loss. Cut the argument step: $0 — on purpose, so nobody can talk the system into a bad call. Swap the chair for simple voting: −$1,113. Every part earns its seat.

The “check the claim first” step matters most — −1,606, the call that turns a win into a walk.

why the “argue it out” step shows −0 — measured in the engine AND liveWe tested this the hard way, twice. In the deterministic engine, removing the argument changes the result by $0. Then we ran the controlled live version: matched pairs inside real Qwen councils — the chair scores the same judges' positions with the same weights before the argument and after it, so only the argument differs. Result: in 12 recorded trials, no judge was ever argued off their position, and the final call never changed. That is the design working, not a gap: each judge is the only voice for its one question, so there is no one to be talked around — persuasion is simply not a channel here, which is what makes the system immune to a well-argued bad idea. Disagreement gets resolved by the chair weighing the situation instead — and that is causal and measured: flatten the chair's weighting and the council loses $1,123 (row above). why this is fair — and where it stopsThe test worlds came from a different model than the one under test (Qwen), so the system didn't write its own exam — a partial guard against tuning to your own tests. Honest limit: the 10 runs measure how steady a result is within a world, not whether it holds across the space of real worlds — and five worlds from one author (Claude) is a small, correlated sample. why this is a real deltaThe reasoning layer fills bounded parameter slots per situation; the engine validates, clamps, and applies them deterministically. This measures what that adaptivity buys over a static parameter set — a genuine component, not a relabel.

Exhibit F — opponents we didn't write (from the literature) — expandThree classic bots from negotiation research. We win one, tie one, lose one. The loss is real, and shown. Synod's edge only appears when the opponent hides something. That's the problem it was built for.

Computing…

Roughly a tie — and that's the honest, expected result. These opponents hide nothing, so there's no bluff to expose and the council's information machinery buys little: it wins against the stubborn one (patience pays), ties the eager one, and gives a little back on the steady one by scouting an opponent with no secret.

what this test found — and fixedRunning against these opponents exposed a real flaw: the council used to hold out past the final round and let a live offer die (its home worlds were kind about deadlines). We added a deterministic deadline rule — on the last round, take a standing offer above your floor rather than bet it on the other side's patience — and re-ran everything: every existing exhibit is byte-identical, and the council stopped burning deadline deals here. Opponents from outside the project finding a real bug is exactly what external tests are for.

§05Why it matters

Why this is worth money.

When the other side hides their real number, one decider gets played. Five judges arguing it out don't.

One decider makes the same two mistakes in every hard negotiation. It believes what the other side says. And it wants the deal too much to walk away. So it pays for fake threats. And it gives up on winnable deals. The council fixes both. One judge's whole job is to say "prove it." Another's is to say "what if we're wrong?" The chair decides who's right, this time.

Who loses this money today

Anyone who negotiates, again and again, with someone who has a reason to lie. And decides alone.

Buying for a company

The supplier's "best and final offer" usually isn't — the council works out what they'd actually accept before you sign.

Selling & discounts

"We have a cheaper quote from your competitor" is the oldest trick going — test it before you cut your price for it.

Recruiting & job offers

Candidates, clients and counter-offers all come with hidden numbers — is that "better offer" real, or a bargaining move?

Buying & selling companies

The rarest deals with the most at stake — "we have another interested buyer" deserves a test, not a better price.

Insurance payouts

Every claim is a negotiation with someone who knows more than you — small overpayments, thousands of times over, add up.

Ceasefires & standoffs

The war room isn't just a story — "back off or else" is exactly the bluff-or-real decision, with the highest stakes there are.

We put it up against real competition negotiators

There's an international contest where negotiation programs play each other (ANAC). We took real entrants — including its winner. Synod negotiated against them for real. We didn't write these opponents. Nobody knew anyone's limits.

Why each piece is there — tap any one to open

A running guess at who you're facing

The other side is hidden, so the council keeps a running guess from what they do, not what they say — and it can't be talked out of the truth.

Scout only when it's worth the turn

Asking a question costs a turn, so the council only asks when what it'd learn is worth it — the line between finding out and stalling.

Plain code makes the final pick

Simple arithmetic decides, not a model's whim — so it's the same every time, and you can see exactly which judge and weight produced it.

A fair chair settles the disagreement

The judges disagree; the chair settles it by weighing them against the situation — not a vote, not the loudest — and takes no side of its own.

A signed record of every decision

Every decision is signed — the call, the weights, the confidence — so no one can quietly change the record afterward.

Fixed starting points, so anyone can re-run it

Fixed starting points mean anyone can re-run the exact tests and get the exact numbers (npm run reproduce) — "trust us" becomes "run it yourself."

Test worlds written by a different AI

The hardest test worlds were written by a different AI (Claude, not the Qwen it runs on) — so it didn't grade its own homework.

THE HONEST BOUNDARY

The engine is proven. The environment is the remaining assumption.

We test it hard in made-up worlds — whether those match real negotiations is still an open question.

So the honest claim is small: in these worlds the council beats one agent — not "proven in the real world," which would need real data.

Standing on named prior art — ANAC / BOA negotiation agents, Bayesian opponent modeling, and multi-agent debate (Du et al.) — with the exact delta from each stated in the README: many decision procedures + auditable aggregation, situation-driven weighting, and anti-groupthink by design.
