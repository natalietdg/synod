§02The hypothesis, in code

If persuasion isn’t the coordination mechanism, what is?

It decides by disagreement.

Part one — why persuasion fails

Every agent society must answer one question: who wins when the agents disagree? Most say: whoever argues best. Synod says: never the best arguer.

Debate societies agents persuade each other↓ one shared belief↓ decision

§05Why it matters — the cost of committing too early

A bet is worth money when committing early is expensive.

Why this is worth money.

Companies don’t lose money because they’re dumb. They lose it by committing too early — believing a story before checking it.

The problem — and what Synod changes

One decider makes the same two mistakes in every hard negotiation: it believes what the other side says, and it wants the deal too much to walk away. So it pays for fake threats, and gives up on winnable deals.

The council fixes both. One judge's whole job is to say "prove it." Another's is to say "what if we're wrong?" The chair decides who's right — this time.

One failure mode, six surfaces

Someone commits before the uncertainty is resolved. Synod is paid for the gap between what the other side claims and what's true.

“Best and final offer.”

It usually isn't — and procurement signs anyway. The council works out what they'd actually accept, first.

“We have a cheaper quote elsewhere.”

The oldest trick in selling. Test it before you cut your price.

“I have another offer.”

Real, or a bargaining move? Every hire and every counter-offer pays for guessing wrong.

“There's another interested buyer.”

M&A: the rarest deals, the most at stake. That claim deserves a test, not a better price.

“That's what the claim is worth.”

Every payout is a negotiation with someone who knows more than you — small overpayments, thousands of times over.

“Back off — or else.”

The war room isn't just a story. The same bluff-or-real call, at the highest stakes there are.

The price of one resolved bluff is already measured — $3,000 instead of $0 (Exhibit A). Every table above repeats it.

And where the other side hides nothing, there's no gap to be paid for — Synod roughly ties, and we measured that too (Exhibit F). We sell the gap, not magic.

§06Architecture — why it follows from the bet

If the bet is right, the architecture follows from it.

The society decides; the human keeps the gavel.

situation → five judges, in parallel → disagreement, on record → deterministic chair → one committed decision

The key idea: the bet, built as structure — no persuasion channel exists. Five independent judgments, resolved by arithmetic into one decision.

  • If persuasion can't be trusted — therefore the chair is deterministic.
  • If judgments must stay independent — therefore no judge shares another's belief.
  • If a model shouldn't decide — therefore arithmetic commits.
  • If every call must be auditable — therefore seeds are fixed and receipts are signed.

How they discuss and debate

If judgments must stay independent — therefore debate exists to expose disagreement, never to persuade the chair. One exception, and it's bounded: the most-opposed pair exchange one round, and a landed objection may move the defender's own score on the contested action — clamped to ±0.1, on the receipt. So a challenge can flip a razor-thin round; it can never stampede the council. Measured: in 12 live matched-pair trials none was close enough to flip, and removing the chair costs $1,123. Persuasion isn't how Synod reaches agreement. Arbitration is.

The room argues.
The chair reads the terrain.
The decision commits.

After the chair commits, the judge that owned the winning question drafts that part of the operational plan — Probe writes the reconnaissance, Trust the back-channel, Hedge the fallback.

Why use math?

If disagreement is preserved — therefore something rhetoric-proof must resolve it. Arithmetic gives three things a model can't: the same answer every time, immunity to rhetoric, and a visible trail — you can see exactly which judge, at what weight, decided. The models reason. The arithmetic decides.

Each judge's math

If each judge owns one question — therefore each owns one formula:

  • Trust — Bayes' rule. Every move the opponent makes updates the odds of who they are — actions only, talk doesn't count. Trust plays what fits the most likely opponent.
  • Probe — Expected Value of Information. What would knowing more be worth, minus what asking costs? Ask only when it pays.
  • Pressure — immediate payoff, with a discount on the future. How much is on the table right now?
  • Hedge — the minimax blend: part worst case, part expected value. How bad does it get if we're wrong?
  • Frame — long-horizon value, priced against the risk of blowing up the deal.
  • The chair — turns the situation into weights, multiplies each judge's score, and the highest total wins.

Two safety gates guard the edges — walk away when nothing believable beats leaving, and take the sure win on the last round. Then the receipt is signed.
