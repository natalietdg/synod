“Why not just ask one model to weigh five perspectives?” — expand
Because these aren't five opinions. They're five different ways of judging. Three are pure math, not an AI. Probe calculates whether a question is worth asking. Hedge calculates the worst case. Trust calculates who they most likely are. In the live run, the five judges reason on Qwen. The math stays outside them, in code. Code tracks the evidence. Code weighs the five. Code makes the call. One model playing five voices gives five guesses from the same head. And a different answer every time. Synod gives the same answer every time — and shows you which judge changed it.

§05Why it matters

Why this is worth money.

When the other side hides their real number, one decider gets played. Five judges arguing it out don't.

One decider makes the same two mistakes in every hard negotiation: it believes what the other side says, and it wants the deal too much to walk away. So it pays for fake threats, and gives up on winnable deals. The council fixes both. One judge's whole job is to say "prove it." Another's is to say "what if we're wrong?" The chair decides who's right — this time.

Who loses this money today

Anyone who negotiates, again and again, with someone who has a reason to lie. And decides alone.

Buying for a company

"Best and final offer" usually isn't. The council works out what they'd actually accept — before you sign.

Selling & discounts

"We have a cheaper quote elsewhere." The oldest trick going. Test it before you cut your price.

Recruiting & job offers

Every counter-offer comes with hidden numbers. Is that "better offer" real? Or a bargaining move?

Buying & selling companies

The rarest deals. The most at stake. "We have another interested buyer" deserves a test, not a better price.

Insurance payouts

Every claim is a negotiation with someone who knows more than you. Small overpayments add up, thousands of times over.

Ceasefires & standoffs

The war room isn't just a story. "Back off or else" is the same bluff-or-real call. Just with the highest stakes there are.

We put it up against real competition negotiators

There's an international contest where negotiation programs play each other (ANAC). We took real entrants — including its winner. Synod negotiated against them for real. We didn't write these opponents. Nobody knew anyone's limits.

How they discuss and debate

Each judge reads the same move independently, in parallel, so no judge anchors another. They debate to expose disagreement — not to persuade. The chair ignores rhetoric and resolves it from the recorded evidence. Measured: removing the debate changed 0/12 live decisions; removing the chair costs $1,123.

Persuasion is not a channel.
The debate exposes disagreement.
The chair resolves it.

What tasks they do

The council decomposes every decision into five judgment tasks:

  • Trust — infer the opponent's incentives.
  • Pressure — find the immediate leverage.
  • Probe — decide whether more information is worth buying.
  • Frame — protect the long-term position.
  • Hedge — minimise the irreversible downside.

After the chair commits, the judge that owned the winning question drafts that part of the operational plan — Probe writes the reconnaissance, Trust the back-channel, Hedge the fallback.

How the math comes into play

Three deterministic components sit outside the models:

  • Belief — updates who-are-they probabilities from the opponent's actions. Talk can't move it.
  • Question price — computes whether asking is worth a turn, before the council asks.
  • Chair — turns the situation into weights, multiplies each judge's score, picks the winner.
  • Safety gates — walk away when nothing believable beats leaving; take the sure win on the last round.
